2015 Poverty-Achievement Index

A Daily Herald and WBEZ analysis of the link between poverty and school achievement

Even with the new PARCC tests, we continue to find a strong correlation between a school's percentage of low-income students and composite test results.

Elementary school results

The interactive graphic below demonstrates how deeply stratified Illinois schools are by income. It shows the average composite meets/exceeds score for each income range, with 0-9.9% being elementary schools with less than 10% of their student population qualifying as low-income.

Mouse over the chart to see results for each income level by year.

Note: The shaded areas in the chart indicate differences in the elementary tests. Minimum ISAT standards for elementary schools were adjusted by the state starting in 2013. In 2015, the PARCC test replaced the ISATS.

High school results

High school scores show stratification in the same way as elementary schools.

Because of a smaller sample size, we divided high schools into eight groups instead of 10, with 0-12.4% being schools with less than 12.5% of their student population qualifying as low-income. Minimum standards were not adjusted for high schools as they were with for elementary schools.

How we calculated the 2015 Poverty-Achievement Index

The PARCC results provide a more nuanced look at scores with the addition of a middle level - the percent of students approaching the standard of college and career ready. While these students are not meeting or exceeding all standards, they are also not failing.

A recent study commissioned by the state of Massachusetts found that a significant percentage of students scoring in the approaching level were doing well in college.

So we looked at the percentage of students who were meeting, exceeding and approaching standards and found the same stratification of average scores, as shown in the chart below.

Because these results showed the same stratification according to income range, and as much correlation between scores and income, we chose to include the percent of students approaching standards in our index.

We also indexed schools by their meets/exceeds score plus the percentage of students in the approaching level. We then averaged the two indexes for the final Poverty-Achievement Index score.
